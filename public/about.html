MagRad-CG

Magnetospheric Radiometric Cybernetic Garden

An interactive 3D visualization of Earth's magnetic field and magnetosphere, driven by real geophysical models and historical solar wind observations. All physics runs in the browser — no server, no plugins.

How It Works

The visualization is computed in three layers, stacked from the inside out:

1
Internal field — IGRF-14
Earth's main magnetic field is computed from the International Geomagnetic Reference Field (IGRF-14), a degree-13 spherical harmonic model published by NOAA/IAGA. The app evaluates the magnetic potential at each point and integrates B(r,θ,φ) using fourth-order Runge–Kutta (RK4) to trace field lines from seed points on the surface. The spherical harmonic degree slider lets you see how successive multipoles add complexity to the pure tilted dipole.
2
External field — Tsyganenko T01
Solar wind interaction reshapes the field significantly: dayside field lines are compressed by the solar wind ram pressure; nightside lines are stretched into the long magnetotail. The T01 empirical model (Tsyganenko 2002) captures all major current systems — ring current, magnetotail current sheet, Birkeland currents, Chapman-Ferraro compression, and IMF penetration — in a single closed-form evaluation. It is parameterized by dynamic pressure, Dst, IMF By/Bz, and the G1/G2 storm-history indices from the Qin-Denton database. The Shue (1998) model defines the magnetopause boundary.
3
Historical data playback — OMNI2 + WGhour.d
The timeline at the bottom drives the solar wind inputs from real hourly observations spanning 1963 to the present. Data comes from the NASA OMNI2 merged dataset (solar wind speed, density, IMF By/Bz, Dst) combined with G1 and G2 pre-computed from the Qin-Denton database using Robert Weigel's QinDenton Fortran tool. Monthly JSON files (~28 KB each) are lazy-loaded as you navigate, so only the months you visit are fetched.
Radiation belts — The inner and outer Van Allen belts are shown as L-shell isosurfaces computed from the dipole approximation (L = r / RE cos²λm). The L-shell grids use the internal field only; the dipole L approximation breaks down with external fields in the tail and at the magnetopause.

The Magnetosphere and Solar Weather

Earth's magnetic shield

Earth's magnetic field is confined into a teardrop-shaped cavity — the magnetosphere — by the continuous outflow of magnetized plasma from the Sun known as the solar wind. Blowing at 300–800 km/s, the solar wind carries its own embedded magnetic field, the interplanetary magnetic field (IMF). Where the solar wind first encounters Earth's field, a standing shock wave called the bow shock forms at roughly 13–15 RE on the dayside. The boundary where Earth's magnetic pressure balances the solar wind ram pressure is the magnetopause, located at roughly 10–11 RE under quiet conditions. On the nightside the field cannot balance the dynamic pressure, and is dragged out hundreds of RE downstream into the long magnetotail, with two lobes of nearly antiparallel field separated by a thin current sheet.

This asymmetry is visible in the field line tubes in this visualization: dayside lines are short and tightly arched; nightside lines are drawn far into the tail. The magnetopause shape itself can be displayed via Solar Wind → Show Magnetopause.

The key driver: IMF Bz

The most important parameter governing how strongly the solar wind couples to Earth is the north-south component of the IMF (Bz). When Bz is northward — parallel to Earth's dayside field — coupling is weak and the magnetosphere is relatively closed. When Bz turns southward, the antiparallel field geometry at the magnetopause nose enables magnetic reconnection: field lines break and re-connect across the boundary, opening a direct channel for solar wind plasma and energy to flow into the magnetosphere. Sustained southward Bz is the single greatest predictor of geomagnetic storm severity, and is the primary input that drives ring current inflation as measured by the Dst index.

Field lines versus L-shell surfaces

Two distinct representations appear in this visualization, answering different questions:

①
Field line tubes (Show Field Lines in the control panel) trace the actual path of the magnetic field vector B(r), computed by fourth-order Runge–Kutta integration of the combined IGRF-14 + T01 field. They show the magnetic highways along which particles gyrate and bounce. With solar wind enabled the tubes are visibly asymmetric: compressed and arched on the dayside, stretched into the nightside tail. The shape responds in real time to the solar wind sliders and to historical data during playback.
②
L-shell surfaces (the solid radiation-belt shells and the transparent isosurfaces under Isosurfaces → L-shell field topology) show surfaces of constant McIlwain L — a parameter that approximates the equatorial distance (in RE) at which the local field line would cross the equatorial plane in a dipole field. These surfaces represent drift shells: a trapped particle does not stay on a single field line but drifts azimuthally around Earth, sweeping through a full ring of field lines. L tells you which drift orbit the particle occupies, not which field line it sits on. In a pure dipole these shells are perfect tori; with T01 enabled they distort, and during severe storms the outer boundary can be clipped by the magnetopause.
Limitation of the L approximation. McIlwain L is derived from the local field direction using the dipole formula L = r / (RE cos²λm). It is a good approximation in the inner magnetosphere (L < 4) but degrades in the distorted outer belt and magnetotail where the field topology departs strongly from a dipole. The rigorous equivalent, Roederer L*, requires tracing the full drift orbit in the 3D field — computationally expensive and outside the scope of this real-time app (Roederer & Lejosne 2018).

Magnetopause shadowing

When the compressed magnetopause crosses inside the outermost drift shells, electrons on those shells encounter the boundary on every azimuthal drift pass and escape into the solar wind. This process — magnetopause shadowing — can deplete electrons at L > 5–6 by 60–99% within a few hours of storm onset (Herrera et al. 2016; Staples et al. 2022). In this visualization, with the Severe Storm solar wind preset enabled, the outer belt L-shell surface is cut open on the dayside, leaving an incomplete torus: the open region represents drift shells that particles cannot maintain without crossing the magnetopause and being lost. The belt recovers when the solar wind pressure relents and new particles are injected on the nightside.

Whether a given storm ultimately enhances or depletes the outer belt depends on which process dominates — injection and wave acceleration, or loss to the magnetopause and atmosphere. This stochastic competition is an active area of research and explains why no two geomagnetic storms produce the same radiation belt outcome.

Radiation Belt Particle Physics

What the particle animation shows — and what it doesn't. The animated dots represent the azimuthal drift of trapped charged particles around Earth — the slowest of three nested periodic motions. They do not show individual particle trajectories. In reality, each particle simultaneously gyrates around a field line millions of times per second, bounces back and forth between mirror points many times per second, and slowly drifts around Earth over tens of minutes. Only the drift is animated here because it is the motion that builds the ring current and determines the large-scale structure of the radiation belts.

Three nested periodic motions

Every trapped particle executes three independent periodic motions, each faster than the next:

1
Gyration — around a magnetic field line, at the particle's Larmor radius.
Period: microseconds to milliseconds (MHz gyrofrequency). The gyroradius of a 1 MeV electron in Earth's inner belt is only a few kilometres — far too small and fast to animate. Gyration is averaged over and not shown.
2
Bounce — back and forth along the field line between magnetic mirror points in each hemisphere, where the increasing field strength reflects the particle.
Period: 0.1–10 seconds. In the visualization, bounce is represented by a static spread in magnetic latitude (λm) up to each particle's mirror point — particles are distributed along field lines but do not oscillate, since the drift is the physically significant motion for the belt structure.
3
Azimuthal drift — slowly around Earth due to the gradient and curvature of the dipole field.
Period: ~16 minutes (1 MeV electrons at L = 4) to several hours (lower energy or larger L).
This is what the animation shows. Electrons drift eastward (counterclockwise viewed from the north pole); protons drift westward (clockwise). The drift periods in the animation are accelerated roughly 6× from physical rates so the motion is clearly visible.

The ring current

The opposite drift directions of electrons and protons constitute the ring current — a toroidal electric current circling Earth at roughly L = 3–6. It is not a current of individual charges moving in circles; it is the net statistical drift of many particles whose gyration and bounce average out, leaving only the slow azimuthal motion. The Dst geomagnetic index directly measures the ring current: more negative Dst means a stronger ring current and a more disturbed magnetic field at Earth's surface. During a geomagnetic storm the ring current can depress the equatorial surface field by 100–400 nT.

Storm-time injection

In quiet times the radiation belts are slowly replenished from the solar wind and from substorm activity. During geomagnetic storms, the compressed magnetotail on the nightside (midnight sector) injects large populations of plasma-sheet particles inward, rapidly energising them and enhancing the ring current. New particles in this visualization therefore appear on the nightside (the side of Earth facing away from the Sun) and then drift around to fill the belt — matching the real injection geometry. The injection rate and target L-shell both scale with the Dst index.

Aurora connection

Particles whose equatorial pitch angle falls below the loss cone angle (αLC, a few degrees at large L) are no longer magnetically trapped. They spiral down field lines into the upper atmosphere at 80–300 km altitude, colliding with oxygen and nitrogen atoms and producing the aurora. The foot of a field line at L ≈ 5–7 reaches the Earth's surface at roughly 65–75° geomagnetic latitude — the auroral oval. Storm-time particle loss into the loss cone therefore appears as a brightening aurora at high latitudes. The aurora oval in this visualization brightens with the magnitude of the Dst index.

Live coupling to historical solar wind data

The particle injection rate and the aurora brightness are not fixed — they respond in real time to the Dst geomagnetic index drawn from the historical solar wind record. As the timeline advances, the app reads hourly Dst values from the same OMNI2-derived dataset that drives the magnetic field model, so the belt and aurora activity you see reflects what was actually happening in near-Earth space at that moment.

Finding active periods with the timeline

The most dramatic belt and aurora activity corresponds to large geomagnetic storms. Use the timeline bar at the bottom of the screen to navigate to dates with historically intense activity. Some notable events in the dataset:

  • March 1989 — one of the strongest storms of the modern era (Dst ≈ −589 nT), responsible for the Quebec power-grid blackout.
  • October–November 2003 ("Halloween storms") — two back-to-back X-class flares with Dst reaching −383 nT; aurora visible at mid-latitudes across North America and Europe.
  • September 2017 — X9.3 flare (the largest of Solar Cycle 24), Dst ≈ −142 nT; aurora sightings as far south as Texas.
  • May 2024 — first severe storm (G5) in twenty years, Dst ≈ −412 nT; widespread aurora across the northern and southern hemispheres.

Navigate to any of these dates with the day buttons or by dragging the playhead, then set the speed to 60× or 3600× to watch the belt fill and drain over hours. Enable Belt Particles and Aurora in the control panel to see the full effect. The Dst readout in the Solar Wind panel updates automatically as the timeline advances.
